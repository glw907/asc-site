## Q1 — Bailment: does an unsecured, self-service lot create one?

Elements (settled): (1) delivery of possession, (2) acceptance of custody, (3)
understanding of return (Cornell LII; Saylor/LibreTexts). No ALI Restatement of
Bailments exists; the field runs on common law and treatises.

The controlling variable is CONTROL, and self-service lots land on the license/lease
side. Self-storage guidance is explicit: no bailment because the renter controls
access (Inside Self-Storage, "The Danger of Creating a Bailment"). Cases: Sackett v.
Public Storage Management (Cal. Ct. App. 1990) (contract's license/lease
characterization controls); DeLaney v. Assured Self Storage (Tex. App. 2008)
(bailment theory rejected). Marinas track the same for wet slips: a slip rental is a
lease of real property with no dominion over the boat (MWL Law marina subrogation
survey). The line flips to bailment when the facility exercises control (valet or
staff-held keys), which shifts the burden of proof to the facility (Washington & Lee
Law Review survey).

Alaska: negligent bailment is recognized (Civil Pattern Jury Instructions art. 21);
no Alaska case squarely on an unsecured multi-vehicle self-park lot. AS 45.07.204
(UCC warehouseman duty) governs commercial warehousemen taking custody, not this
pattern. The stakes: a bailee faces a PRESUMPTION of negligence once damage is shown
(Marquette Law Review); a licensor does not — the claimant must prove negligence.
Hence the agreement should affirmatively state no bailment is created.

Bottom line: on these facts (unfenced, unattended, members keep keys, club never
touches the property) the arrangement sits on the license side. Flag: no Alaska case
directly on point.

## Q2 — What marinas, yacht clubs, and storage facilities actually use

Standalone bilateral storage/dockage agreements, never a folded-in waiver (Chubb
marina guidance; templates: Marina Jack, Four Winds Marina, Barber Marina, Affordable
Marine Care). "Sole risk" exculpatory clauses are near-universal, construed strictly
against the drafter; enforceability against the facility's own negligence splits by
federal circuit (5th/9th permissive; 11th/1st restrictive) (MWL Law).
Owner-to-facility indemnification appears in every template surveyed (fire spread,
third-party damage). Owner-carried insurance commonly required ($300K-$1M liability
plus casualty), some naming the facility as additional insured, others
certificate-only. Lien/abandonment clauses are settled practice, contractual notice
periods 10-30 days, layered on or replacing state lien statutes. Wet vs. dry storage:
mixed practice, no dominant convention on one agreement vs. two.

## Q3 — Enforceability of property-damage exculpation (Alaska + general)

Courts are somewhat more permissive toward property-damage exculpation than
personal-injury waivers (Virginia bars injury waivers but allows property releases;
bailment treatises note judges are "slightly more apt to enforce" property clauses),
still refusing gross negligence/intentional conduct. Alaska's controlling framework
is the personal-injury line applied by analogy: Kissick v. Schmierer, 816 P.2d 188
(Alaska 1991); Moore v. Hartley Motors, 36 P.3d 628 (Alaska 2001); Ledgends v. Kerr,
91 P.3d 960 (Alaska 2004); Donahue v. Ledgends, 331 P.3d 342 (Alaska 2014) (the
six-factor test); Langlois v. Nova River Runners (2018, memorandum). NOTE: secondary
sources claim Alaska applies the Tunkl factors, but a direct read of Donahue found it
does not cite Tunkl by name — attorney verification item. No Alaska case applying an
exculpatory clause to property damage was found: a genuine gap; counsel argues by
analogy. Cutting the other way: AS 45.45.900 voids sole-negligence property-damage
indemnification in construction contracts — narrow, but shows the legislature does
not treat property exculpation as automatically permissive.

Nonprofit status: AS 09.65.170 grants qualifying nonprofit directors/officers
immunity absent gross negligence — protects individual volunteer leaders, NOT the
club entity. No general charitable immunity in Alaska.

Drafting takeaways (convergent): use "negligence" explicitly; name specific risks
(theft, weather, fire); clear conspicuous simple language; no bare "at owner's risk"
catch-all alone; never represent a security standard not met; gross negligence and
intentional misconduct non-waivable regardless.

## Q4 — Risks the club carries without an agreement

- Fire spreading between stored boats/RVs: real and litigated. Sunny Isles Marina v.
  Adulami, 706 So. 2d 920 (Fla. Dist. Ct. App. 1998): dry-lot fire; the marina's own
  exculpatory clause was struck as internally contradictory. Lesson: an overreaching
  or inconsistent clause can void entirely. Fire risk is structurally elevated on
  boat/RV lots (fuel, propane, lithium batteries).
- Club-caused damage moving a trailer: real; gross-negligence carve-out applies; a
  member's auto policy typically does not cover items on a trailer.
- Theft/vandalism on an unsecured lot: litigated as "negligent security"; liability
  commonly turns on advertised security not maintained. An unsecured lot making no
  security representations reduces exposure.
- Weather/tree-fall: general premises liability; attaches where a hazardous condition
  was known or knowable.
- Abandonment/removal costs: real; Alaska statutory coverage partial (Q6).

What the agreement actually mitigates: theft/vandalism/weather via clean
exculpation + assumption of risk (no club conduct implicated); fire-spread and
club-operations damage partially (disclosure + member indemnification, but not the
club's own gross negligence); abandonment/lien nearly entirely, and it is close to
essential given the statutory gaps.

## Q5 — Does the general release cover stored property? Is a separate agreement standard?

A release framed around activity participation should not be assumed to discharge
property-storage claims: storage is not "participation," and waiver scope is
construed narrowly against the drafter (consistent with Kissick). A maritime
attorney's review of 15 yacht clubs' waiver language found roughly two-thirds
deficient from copy-pasted templates (The Log). Two separate instruments is
doctrinally grounded: an activity release and a storage arrangement are different
duty-of-care regimes (LibreTexts, Bailments); self-storage industry guidance
independently recommends the same split and recommends affirmatively disclaiming
bailment (Inside Self-Storage). Owner-carried insurance requirement: facilities'
liability policies don't cover customers' property; industry guidance recommends
proof of the owner's own coverage as a storage condition. Annual re-sign: common by
analogy to membership-cycle waiver renewal (RRCA guidance); no storage-specific
authority found — inference, supported by the changing member/item mix.

## Q6 — Abandoned property and liens in Alaska

CITATION ERROR CAUGHT AND CORRECTED: one research pass initially cited an Alaska
self-storage lien statute at "AS 34.35.600-670." Verification against the
legislature's bill tracker shows that is BILL TEXT from HB 97 (33rd Legislature),
which passed the House 27-13 on 2024-03-21 and DIED IN THE SENATE — never enacted.
Alaska is described in industry sources as the last state without a self-storage
lien law. Counsel should re-verify current status at drafting time. (HB 97's own
"self-storage facility" definition required renter-controlled locked units, so an
open club lot likely wouldn't have qualified regardless.)

What Alaska actually has:

- AS 28.11 — abandoned titled vehicles/trailers (a boat trailer is a registered
  vehicle): 30+ days without consent presumed abandoned; landowner may claim after
  notice to owner of record and lienholders; title vests if unclaimed. AS 28.11.090
  ties into DMV lien sales. The most on-point statute for RVs and bare trailers.
- AS 30.30 — vessels: derelict vessels trigger state/municipal impoundment (not a
  private disposal right); abandoned vessels (30+ days, AS 30.30.170(5)) get a
  notice-and-sale procedure (AS 30.30.100-.140), but that article's title limits it
  to businesses "engaged in [vessel] repair" — whether a member-storage-only
  nonprofit falls inside is unconfirmed; attorney verification item.
- AS 34.35.220/.225 — the general storage lien, the strongest currently codified
  theory: possessory lien for reasonable charges; sale at public auction after three
  months unpaid, limited to covering the debt; statutory penalty (market value +50%)
  for unauthorized sale. General-commerce law, untested on this pattern, but real.

Contract fills the gap (compare Maine's Marina and Boatyard Storage Act, Title 10
ch. 212-A, and Michigan's Act 362 of 1998, which name yacht clubs; Alaska has no
equivalent): a private lien clause, a deemed-abandoned trigger after stated
nonpayment plus notice, and a removal/sale right with proceeds applied to the debt.
Conversion risk if done without authority: conversion is a strict-liability tort;
good faith is no defense; the right to sell is purely statutory or contractual,
never inherent.

## Recommendation

(a) A dedicated stored-property agreement is warranted, as a separate bilateral
contract: different body of law, universal industry practice, and Alaska's statutory
gaps require contractual lien/abandonment authority no release supplies.

(b) Clause checklist: (1) no-bailment disclaimer (self-service, no custody, member
retains access/control); (2) assumption of risk naming theft, vandalism, weather
(wind, snow load, tree fall), fire including fire from another member's property,
and club lot operations, with "negligence" explicit; (3) truthful statement the lot
is unsecured — no fence, attendant, or monitoring; (4) exculpation scoped to
ordinary negligence, never overreaching (the Sunny Isles trap); (5) member-to-club
indemnification for damage the member's property causes; (6) owner's-own-insurance
proof as a storage condition (additional-insured vs. certificate is a club call);
(7) contractual lien + deemed-abandoned trigger + notice (certified mail) + removal/
sale right, attorney-checked against AS 34.35.220/.225 and AS 28.11, conversion risk
flagged; (8) club-operations clause for consented trailer moves, without implying a
duty of care that undercuts (1); (9) fresh signature each season tied to the storage
fee.

(c) The mooring document should carry equivalent property-risk substance: a boat on
a club mooring is stored property, and moorings are more bailment-prone wherever the
club controls tackle; whether one mooring agreement or a release plus property rider
is the attorney's drafting choice.

(d) Attorney questions: does Donahue govern property-only exculpation or apply only
by analogy; does AS 30.30 art. 2 cover a non-repair nonprofit; Tunkl's actual status
in Alaska; any AS 45.45.900-style skepticism imported into storage indemnification;
current self-storage lien bill status; AS 09.65.170 scope and any companion
volunteer-immunity statute; additional-insured vs. certificate, and the abandonment
notice period (industry range 14-90 days).
