Racing at the ASC picks up where our sailing classes leave off, giving experienced sailors a fleet to plug into when they move to Anchorage. Graduates of our [Introduction to Dinghy Sailing](/education/) course step straight into real racing alongside veteran competitors who remember being new. Whether you're after relaxed weeknight racing, tight one-design starts, or a national championship, the program scales to meet you.

## The Season

Our season runs Memorial Day through September. It starts informal and stays that way for most sailors: Wednesday evening races, weekend regattas with potlucks and camping, a pirate-themed scavenger hunt, and a season closer that uses islands as marks. If you just finished a beginner class, Wet Wednesdays are the natural next step.

:::passage[Wet Wednesdays]{icon="waves"}
Our midweek evening series and the easiest way to start racing. Sailors arrive at 6:00 p.m., with racing starting around 7:00 p.m. Short windward/leeward courses, rabbit start format. No regatta scoring, no formal skippers meeting: just show up and sail.

New sailors are always welcome. We find everyone a spot on a boat, usually crewing for an experienced skipper who can talk you through the race in real time. Everything you practiced in Introduction to Dinghy Sailing, from tacking and jibing to right-of-way, shows up here at a slightly faster pace.

Six designated Wednesdays form a summer-long high-point series. Dates are on the [Events page](/events/).
:::

:::passage[Weekend Regattas]{icon="flag-pennant"}
The season includes four weekend regattas: the Icebreaker, Firecracker, Fireweed Ladies Race, and Governor's Cup. Dates and details are on the [Events page](/events/).

More structured than Wednesdays. On Saturday, the skippers meeting starts at 1:00 p.m. Racing follows when conditions allow. Sunday's meeting is at 11:00 a.m. We aim for five to seven races over two days with formal scoring across multiple fleets.

Regattas are where you start to feel the rhythm of racing: skippers meetings, starting sequences, mark roundings, and a potluck after Saturday's races. If you've done a few Wet Wednesdays, you're ready. The same people who helped you on Wednesday will be on the starting line Saturday.
:::

:::passage[The Pirate Race]{icon="skull"}
Not really a race. The Pirate Race is the biggest on-water scavenger hunt and water fight in Alaska. It's the club's signature party event, and the rules are rarely the regular rules of sailing. People dress as pirates (not required, but the costumes are spectacular). Creative cheating and skullduggery are strongly encouraged.

If you're nervous about racing, this is the perfect first event. Nobody is keeping score in any meaningful sense, and you'll meet half the club.
:::

:::passage[The Northern Lights Race]{icon="moon-stars"}
Racing on Saturday, work party on Sunday. The Northern Lights weekend is how ASC closes its season in two acts. Race day is quiet and low-key, with islands as marks instead of buoys and a full-club campfire afterward. Northern Lights are optional but frequently delivered. On Sunday, the whole club reconvenes for the annual work party: pulling out docks, winterizing equipment, and putting the place to bed until spring.
:::

:::passage[Buccaneer North American Championship (BNAC)]{icon="trophy"}
The BNAC rotates to a different host club each year, including Alaska. ASC hosted the 2025 BNAC on Big Lake and sends sailors to the championship annually, wherever it lands. If you're racing the Buccaneer Fleet competitively, this is the marquee event on the calendar.
:::

## From Class to Starting Line

Many of our Buccaneer Fleet sailors started in a beginner class. The path from "What's a tiller?" to one-design racing is shorter than you'd think.

[Introduction to Dinghy Sailing](/education/) ends with racing drills and mock starts; Day 4 is specifically designed to give you what you need for your first real race. From there, you move into the Open Fleet, where experienced sailors help new racers find their footing, usually crewing for a veteran skipper before helming on your own. The structure is deliberate: the education program builds the skills, the racing program puts them to work.

You don't have to be fast. You don't have to know the rulebook. You just have to be willing to go out and try. The people you raced against in class drills on Sunday will be the same people helping you rig a boat on Wednesday evening.

### Your First Race

Plan to arrive **two to three hours before the skippers meeting**. You'll need time to rig a boat, find someone to sail with if you don't have a crew or skipper yet, and, most importantly, get some practice sailing in before the racing starts. A few laps around the bay before the gun goes off does more for your confidence than any amount of reading.

The skippers meeting covers the course, any special instructions, and fleet starting order. Tell someone it's your first race. You'll get paired with a boat or pointed to a club boat, and people will answer every question you have.

For Wet Wednesdays, it's even simpler. There's no skippers meeting. Arrive a bit before 6:00 p.m. to rig and get on the water, let people know you're new, and you'll be comfortable well before the 7:00 p.m. start.

### What to Bring

Weather on Big Lake runs the full range: 50°F, windy, and rainy to 80°F and sunny, sometimes in the same afternoon. Pack for both.

| Item | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Layers and foul weather gear | A jacket and pants that can handle wind and spray are the most important items on this list. |
| Dry clothes | Leave them in your car. You'll be glad they're there. |
| Sunglasses and sunscreen | The sun reflects hard off the water even on overcast days. |
| Water and snacks | Races run longer than you expect and the clubhouse isn't always accessible mid-event. |
| Watch with countdown timer | Essential for timing your start. Any sports watch works. |
| Handheld VHF radio | Required for the Buccaneer Fleet; strongly recommended for the Open Fleet. The race committee communicates on Channel 68. |

## Racing Fleets

Racing is organized into fleets: groups of boats that start together and are scored together. The fleet system lets us run fair races across a wide range of boats and experience levels on the same course at the same time.

### Open Fleet

Any boat type is welcome. Scoring uses the Portsmouth Yardstick handicap system, which adjusts finishing times based on each boat's design speed. A Sunfish can race against a Laser and the results mean something. This is where most people start racing, and where many choose to stay. The fleet is genuinely mixed: first-season sailors on the same starting line as twenty-year veterans.

You don't need to own a boat. Club boats available to Open Fleet sailors include Lido 14s, Lasers, a Catalina 16.5, a Skipjack, and a Buccaneer 18.

### Buccaneer 18 Fleet

Sixteen boats, the largest individual Buccaneer 18 fleet in North America. One-design racing where boat handling and tactics decide the results, not equipment differences. Starts and mark roundings get loud. The club hosted the 2025 BNAC and sends sailors to the championship annually. If you want close racing with a national competitive pathway, this is it.

Many new sailors get their first Buccaneer experience crewing for an experienced skipper. It's a great way to learn the boat, but the Buccaneer Fleet isn't the ideal place to skipper your first race. Start in the Open Fleet, build your starts and mark roundings there, then move up.

### Ad Hoc Fleets

If three or more boats of the same type show up, they can request separate one-design scoring. Notify the race committee at the skippers meeting before the first race. Ad hoc fleets start with the Open Fleet but are scored against each other.

## How Racing Works

A sailboat race is simpler to follow than it looks. Boats start together, sail a set course, and the first one across the finish line wins. Once you've seen it a couple of times, the rhythm clicks quickly.

### The Start

The most surprising part for newcomers: there's no "on your marks, go." Instead, the race committee runs a countdown sequence using flags and horn signals from the committee boat, typically five minutes. During that time, boats maneuver behind the starting line, building speed. The goal is to cross the line at full sail the instant the final horn sounds.

Cross early and you have to circle back behind the line and re-cross it. That costs you a lot. Getting the start right is one of the hardest skills in racing, and one of the most satisfying to improve at. Don't worry about it on your first day. Ask the skipper you're crewing with to explain what they're seeing. You'll learn more from one start than from any amount of reading.

### The Course

At ASC, most races use a windward/leeward course: sail upwind to a mark, round it, sail downwind to the next mark, and repeat for a set number of laps. The race committee sets the marks before racing starts, using wind direction to lay a fair upwind leg.

If you've done Days 2 and 3 of Introduction to Dinghy Sailing, you already know how to sail the course. Racing adds precision: tighter tacks, better angles, greater awareness of the boats around you. The course itself is what you practiced.

### The Finish and Scoring

Cross the finish line between the committee boat and the finish mark. The committee records your time. In the Open Fleet, finishing times are adjusted by handicap so different boat types are compared fairly. In the Buccaneer Fleet, first across the line wins.

Over a series or a multi-race weekend, each race earns points: first place gets one, second gets two, and so on. Lowest total wins. Most weekends, your worst result gets dropped, so one rough race doesn't define your regatta.

### Right-of-Way on the Course

You covered the basics of right-of-way in the beginner class. Racing uses the same rules, just faster and closer together. The three you'll use constantly:

- A boat on **starboard tack** has right of way over a boat on port tack.
- The **windward boat** keeps clear of the leeward boat when both are on the same tack.
- A boat **overtaking from behind** must keep clear of the boat ahead.

If you break a rule, take a penalty turn on the spot and keep sailing. Everyone fouls someone eventually.

## Racing Communication

Racing runs on a short list of documents and channels. Before your first event, knowing where things live is more useful than studying the rules.

:::passage[Notice of Race and Sailing Instructions]{icon="newspaper"}
Every regatta is announced with a **Notice of Race (NOR)**: the schedule, format, and entry details, published in advance. On race day, the **Sailing Instructions (SI)** govern what happens on the water, the specific course, the starting sequence, how scoring works, and any special rules for that event. Both documents are posted on this site. Links go out in `#racing` on Discord and via email, typically at least a day or two before the event, so you can read through the SI at home rather than scrambling on the dock the morning of.
:::

:::passage[The Skippers Meeting]{icon="compass"}
Every race day begins on shore with the skippers meeting, run by the **Principal Race Officer (PRO)**, who leads the race committee. The PRO describes the course, covers any special instructions, and takes questions. If multiple fleets are racing, the meeting includes the starting order so every fleet knows when to get on the water.

For new racers, the skippers meeting is the most useful five minutes of the day. Ask questions. Veterans do too.

For Wet Wednesdays, there is no skippers meeting. Arrive by 6:00 p.m. to rig up and get on the water before the 7:00 p.m. start.
:::

:::passage[VHF Channel 68]{icon="radio"}
The race committee monitors **VHF Channel 68** throughout racing. Course changes, postponements, weather calls, and safety messages all go out over the radio. A handheld VHF marine radio is required for Buccaneer Fleet races and strongly recommended for the Open Fleet.
:::

:::passage[The #racing Channel]{icon="chats"}
`#racing` on Discord is the hub for all racing communication outside of race day: regatta announcements, NOR and SI links, results after each event, and general discussion. If you want to know what's coming up, it's the first place to look.
:::

## Beyond the Basics

Like ogres and onions, sailboat racing has layers.

The start sequence, the course, the finish: those are the frame. But underneath them is a world of questions that will start appearing after your first few races, almost without you noticing. Why did that boat tack when it did? How did they get so far ahead at the windward mark when we crossed the line together? Why does the fleet suddenly all seem to be heading left?

These are questions about **wind shifts**: the constant small (and sometimes large) changes in wind direction that determine which side of the course is favored. Reading them is what separates an experienced racer from a beginner, not boat speed.

Laylines. Tactical positioning at mark roundings. The rules as a tool, not just a constraint; knowing when another boat owes you room, and how to claim it confidently. Sail trim: small adjustments to leech tension or sail twist that change how fast you're moving without touching the rudder. The moment it all starts to come together in real time is one of the better feelings in the sport.

The club's teaching culture doesn't stop at the end of the beginner class. Experienced sailors debrief after races and share what they saw on the course. [Fleet Tune-Up Weekend](/education/) each June is specifically designed to sharpen racing skills before the season hits full stride. Ask questions on `#racing`. People will answer.
